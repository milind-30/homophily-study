# Cochran–Armitage Test Results

A series of Cochran–Armitage tests assessed whether the probability of choosing the more-similar “focal” character increased in a linear fashion with similarity difference (similarity difference score = 0, 1, 2, or 3). Across all participants, there was a significant positive trend, Z = 2.334, p = .020, indicating that, in general, as the number of shared features rose, participants were increasingly likely to choose the focal character.

Splitting the data by Task revealed that the linear trend was significant for the Pairwise Choice condition (Z = 2.426, p = .015), whereas there was no evidence of a trend under Full Ranking (Z = 0.883, p = .377). A similar split by Condition (Casual vs. Competitive) showed neither to be strictly significant. The Competitive framing approached but did not reach significance (Z = 1.539, p = .124), and the Casual framing showed a borderline trend (Z = 1.777, p = .076).

Further subdividing by Task × Condition revealed that Pairwise Choice under Casual framing showed a robust linear pattern (Z = 2.395, p = .017). By contrast, the three other Task–Condition combinations (Pairwise Choice–Competitive, Full Ranking–Competitive, and Full Ranking–Casual) showed no significant linear trend, all ps > .05.

Taken together, these results suggest that participants' tendency to select more-similar characters is especially evident when they make pairwise comparisons, particularly under a Casual framing, whereas the trend is less evident in the Full Ranking format or under a Competitive framing.
